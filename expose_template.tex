\section{Introduction and Motivation}

Industrial production systems are characterized by high complexity due to fluctuating workloads and limited resource availability. A central challenge is the Flexible Job-Shop Scheduling Problem (FJSS), an NP-hard optimization task that involves two coupled decision levels: machine assignment (routing operations to capable resources) and operation sequencing (ordering tasks on each machine) to minimize the overall completion time, known as makespan ($C_{max}$).

Traditional approaches rely on predefined heuristics or rule-based strategies (e.g., SPT or EDD)~\cite[Chapter 3]{leungHandbookSchedulingAlgorithms2004}. While robust, these methods often lack the flexibility required for complex, multi-objective environments.

Advances in artificial intelligence have introduced new approaches for solving complex decision-making problems. Reinforcement learning (RL) has shown promising results for dynamic scheduling~\cite{WASCHNECK20181264, waschneckDeepReinforcementLearning2018, zhangDeepReinforcementLearning2023}, but typically requires extensive training and retraining when system conditions change.

Recent advances in Large Language Models (LLMs) have shown significant potential in reasoning and planning. Unlike Reinforcement Learning (RL), which requires extensive training for specific environments, LLM-based agents offer the possibility of zero-shot generalization.

This work evaluates whether the inherent reasoning capabilities of LLMs can be leveraged to solve FJSS instances without any domain-specific fine-tuning or training. The focus lies on understanding the gap between LLM-generated schedules and mathematical optima.


\section{State of the Art}

Classical scheduling approaches include rule-based heuristics and exact optimization methods aimed at minimizing metrics such as makespan or waiting time~\cite[Chapter 3]{leungHandbookSchedulingAlgorithms2004}. While exact methods guarantee optimality, their computational complexity often limits them to smaller instances.

Current research explores LLMs as agents capable of "Reasoning and Acting" (ReAct)~\cite{yaoREACSYNERGIZINGREASONING2022}. While initial applications in industrial control exist~\cite{xiaControlIndustrialAutomation2025b, vyasAutonomousIndustrialControl2024a}, the application to FJSS remains largely unexplored. This research distinguishes itself from closely related works (e.g., ~\cite{abgaryanLLMsCanSchedule2024}) by focusing strictly on the zero-shot performance of frontier models and providing a rigorous comparison against exact solvers (CP-SAT) to quantify the optimality gap.


\section{Problem Definition and Research Questions}

The goal of this research is to evaluate the ability of LLM-based agents to handle the combinatorial complexity of FJSS (assignment and sequencing) under zero-shot conditions. The primary objective is the minimization of the makespan $C_{max}$ while respecting all precedence and resource constraints.

The research focuses on two central questions:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{RQ1:} To what extent can LLM-based agents generate valid and efficient scheduling decisions in flexible job-shop scenarios (FJSS) without domain-specific training or fine-tuning compared to established heuristics and exact solvers?
    \item \textbf{RQ2:} How do prompt engineering (e.g., Chain-of-Thought) and input representation influence the agent's ability to maintain constraints and reduce the optimality gap?
\end{itemize}

Supporting aspects such as real-time suitability and comparison with classical heuristics (e.g., EDD) are analyzed as secondary considerations.



\section{Planned Methodology}

This work combines a literature review with a experimental evaluation in a simulated production environment. To ensure feasibility and scientific rigor, the study utilizes established benchmarks (e.g., Taillard's instances) and leverages the \texttt{job-shop-lib} for environment simulation.

A simplified flexible job-shop simulation is developed to model a production system consisting of a fixed number of machines and jobs with sequential operations. The simulation focuses on machine allocation and job sequencing decisions. Some operations can be processed on multiple alternative machines, while others require a specific machine. Multiple jobs can be present in the system simultaneously.


\subsection{Benchmarking Strategy}
The performance of the LLM-based agent is evaluated using a three-tier comparison:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Lower Bound (Random Dispatcher):} Serves as a baseline to validate the complexity of the chosen problem instances.
    \item \textbf{Heuristic Baseline (EDD):} Represents the standard industrial heuristic against which the LLM must compete.
    \item \textbf{Optimal Reference (CP-SAT):} Using Google OR-Tools, the global optimum is calculated for small to medium-sized instances to determine the absolute performance quality of the LLM.
\end{enumerate}

Multiple runs per configuration are conducted to analyze stability and reproducibility.


\subsection{Experimental Design}

The LLM-based agent receives a structured representation of the machine environment (both in natural language and JSON) and the jobs to be processed (including their operations, processing times, and due dates). Based on this input, the LLM generates a series of decisions interpreted as job-machine assignments. These decisions are validated and executed within the simulation environment.

If time permits, a dynamic scenario with random job arrivals and machine failures will be furthermore explored. Key variables include:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Input representation:} Structured JSON format vs. natural language.
    \item \textbf{Prompt design:} Basic zero-shot instructions vs. Chain-of-Thought (CoT).
\end{itemize}



\subsection{Data}

The simulation is implemented using \texttt{job-shop-lib}, with optima calculated via \texttt{OR-Tools} (\texttt{cp\_model}) and benchmarking using Taillard instances. Synthetic job data is generated to simulate realistic production scenarios. Each job consists of a sequence of operations that must be processed in a predefined order and has an associated due date.

Processing times depend on the job type and the assigned machine. In the optional dynamic scenario, additional variability is introduced through random job arrivals and machine failures.

The system state provided to the LLM includes:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Available machines and their status (idle, busy, unavailable, and expected finish times) 
    \item Jobs waiting for processing
    \item Remaining operations per job
    \item Due dates
\end{itemize}

This state is provided to the LLM in different representations, namely structured JSON format and natural language descriptions.

\subsection{Planned Model / Experiment / Analysis}

The experiments follow a factorial design focusing on the static FJSS to isolate the reasoning capabilities of the models. The agent's task is to observe the system state and output a machine-readable set of job-machine assignments.

Valid and conflict-free actions are executed, while invalid or conflicting actions are discarded and recorded for evaluation. To reduce complexity, the model abstracts transport processes by assigning fixed transfer times between machines instead of explicitly modeling routing decisions. Multiple jobs can be processed in parallel, depending on machine availability.


The experiments follow a factorial design with the following factors:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Scenario: Static vs. Dynamic (optional)
    \item Input representation: JSON vs. Text
    \item Prompt design: Basic vs. Chain-of-Thought
\end{itemize}

Each experimental configuration is repeated multiple times to ensure robustness of the results. The analysis compares the LLM performance across configurations and against the heuristic baseline, including a qualitative analysis of typical error patterns.

\subsection{Planned Evaluation}
The evaluation is based on standard performance metrics from production scheduling and LLM-specific metrics:

\textbf{Efficiency and Scheduling Metrics:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Optimality Gap:} The relative difference between the LLM's $C_{max}$ and the CP-SAT optimum.
    \item \textbf{Heuristic Improvement:} Performance gain/loss relative to the EDD baseline.
    \item \textbf{Makespan ($C_{max}$):} Total completion time.
    \item \textbf{Tardy Jobs:} Number of jobs exceeding their due date.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{LLM-specific and Robustness Metrics:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Feasibility Rate:} Rate of invalid decisions (e.g., precedence violations).
    \item \textbf{Technical Robustness:} Rate of invalid output formats or invalid/conflicting assignments.
    \item \textbf{Latency:} Response time per decision.
\end{itemize}

In addition to quantitative evaluation, the robustness and consistency of the LLM are analyzed by examining repeated runs under identical conditions. Furthermore, a comparison with the EDD  and CP-SAT heuristic provides a baseline for assessing the effectiveness of the LLM-based approach.
