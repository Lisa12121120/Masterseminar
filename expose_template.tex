\section{Introduction and Motivation}

Industrial production systems are characterized by high complexity due to fluctuating workloads and limited resource availability. A central challenge is the Flexible Job-Shop Scheduling Problem (FJSS), an NP-hard optimization task that involves two coupled decision levels: machine assignment (routing operations to capable resources) and operation sequencing (ordering tasks on each machine) to minimize the overall completion time, known as makespan ($C_{max}$). In the flexible variant, each operation may be processed on one of several eligible machines, each with a different processing time, making the assignment and sequencing decisions tightly coupled.

Traditional approaches rely on predefined heuristics or rule-based strategies (e.g., SPT or EDD)~\cite[Chapter 3]{leungHandbookSchedulingAlgorithms2004}. While robust, these methods often lack the flexibility required for complex, multi-objective environments.

Advances in artificial intelligence have introduced new approaches for solving complex decision-making problems. Reinforcement learning (RL) has shown promising results for dynamic scheduling~\cite{WASCHNECK20181264, waschneckDeepReinforcementLearning2018, zhangDeepReinforcementLearning2023}, but typically requires extensive training and retraining when system conditions change.

Large Language Models (LLMs) offer a fundamentally different paradigm: rather
than learning a policy through thousands of training interactions, they can
reason over a textual description of the scheduling state and generate
decisions without any task-specific training or fine-tuning. This
zero-shot generalization capability distinguishes LLM-based approaches from
both classical heuristics and RL methods, and is the central property
evaluated in this work.

This work therefore asks: can the general reasoning capabilities of LLMs,
applied zero-shot, produce scheduling decisions of sufficient quality to
compete with established heuristics, and how large is the gap to mathematical
optima?


\section{State of the Art}

Classical scheduling approaches include rule-based heuristics and exact optimization methods aimed at minimizing metrics such as makespan or waiting time~\cite[Chapter 3]{leungHandbookSchedulingAlgorithms2004}. While exact methods guarantee optimality, their computational complexity often limits them to smaller instances.

Recent work applies LLMs as agents capable of Reasoning and Acting (ReAct)~\cite{yaoREACSYNERGIZINGREASONING2022}, and initial applications in industrial control systems have been demonstrated~\cite{xiaControlIndustrialAutomation2025,vyasAutonomousIndustrialControl2024}.


The most closely related work is Abgaryan et al.~\cite{abgaryanLLMsCanSchedule2024}, who show that LLMs can solve Job-Shop Scheduling Problems with performance comparable to other neural approaches. However, their approach relies on supervised fine-tuning of LLMs on a purpose-built dataset of 120k scheduling instances. This work differs fundamentally: no training data is used, no model is fine-tuned, and no domain-specific supervision is provided. Instead, frontier LLMs are evaluated purely in a zero-shot setting, directly testing their general reasoning capabilities on FJSS. This distinction is the core contribution and the key open question in the literature.


\section{Problem Definition and Research Questions}

The goal of this research is to evaluate the zero-shot decision-making ability
of LLM-based agents on the FJSS. The primary objective is minimization of the
makespan~$C_{max}$ while respecting all precedence and resource constraints.
The research is focused on one central question with one supporting aspect:
 
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{RQ1 (Core):} To what extent can frontier LLMs generate valid
    and efficient scheduling decisions for the FJSS in a zero-shot setting,
    without any task-specific training or fine-tuning, compared to a random
    dispatcher, an EDD heuristic, and a CP-SAT optimal solver?
  \item \textbf{RQ2 (Supporting):} How do prompt design (zero-shot
    vs.\ Chain-of-Thought) and input representation (JSON vs.\ natural
    language) influence the quality and feasibility of LLM scheduling
    decisions?
\end{itemize}
 
RQ1 addresses the fundamental capability question and provides the main
contribution. RQ2 provides practical guidance on how to best elicit scheduling
decisions from LLMs and is addressed through a focused factorial experiment.

\section{Planned Methodology}

This work combines a literature review with an experimental evaluation in a simulated production environment. To ensure feasibility within the time frame
of the Masterseminar, the implementation maximizes reuse of established
libraries and minimizes custom development. The central design decision is to
use job-shop-lib's Dispatcher~\cite{arinoPabloo22Job_shop_lib2026} as a unified
interface between all solvers: every strategy -- LLM, EDD, Random, and CP-SAT
-- receives the same dispatcher state and returns a scheduling decision. This
makes the strategies interchangeable and limits new development to only the
LLM-specific components.

Concretely, the following components are reused directly from existing
libraries without modification: the simulation environment and dispatching
state management (job-shop-lib Dispatcher), benchmark
instance loading (load\_benchmark\_instance() for Taillard
instances~\cite{taillardBenchmarksBasicScheduling1993}), random instance generation
(GeneralInstanceGenerator), the EDD and random dispatching rules
(DispatchingRuleSolver) and the CP-SAT optimal solver
(ORToolsSolver). LLM inference is conducted locally
via Ollama or through a cloud-based API (e.g., OllamaApi), depending on
model availability and computational resources.

The components developed specifically for this work are limited to: (1)~the
LLM agent bridge, which reads the Dispatcher state and
serializes it into an LLM-readable prompt in either JSON or natural language
format; and (2)~the evaluation pipeline with its associated metrics.

A flexible job-shop simulation is built on top of job-shop-lib to
model a production system with a fixed number of machines and jobs with
sequential operations. Some operations can be processed on multiple alternative
machines, while others require a specific machine. Multiple jobs can be present
in the system simultaneously.


\subsection{Benchmarking Strategy}
The performance of the LLM-based agent is evaluated using a three-tier
comparison:
 
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Lower Bound (Random Dispatcher):} A random dispatching rule
    provided by job-shop-lib serves as a baseline to validate the
    complexity of the chosen problem instances.
  \item \textbf{Heuristic Baseline (EDD):} The Earliest Due Date rule,
    provided by job-shop-lib's DispatchingRuleSolver,
    represents the standard industrial heuristic against which the LLM must
    compete.
  \item \textbf{Optimal Reference (CP-SAT):} The global optimum is computed
    via the ORToolsSolver included in job-shop-lib for small
    to medium-sized instances to determine the absolute performance quality of
    the LLM.
\end{enumerate}
 
Multiple runs per configuration are conducted to analyze stability and
reproducibility.


\subsection{Experimental Design}

The LLM-based agent receives a structured representation of the scheduling
state via the Dispatcher interface at each decision step. This
includes machine status (idle, busy, and expected finish times), all waiting
jobs with remaining operations, processing times per eligible machine, and due
dates. Based on this input, the LLM generates one job-machine assignment per
step, which is validated and executed via Dispatcher.dispatch().
Invalid actions are recorded for evaluation and replaced by an EDD fallback
to ensure the simulation always terminates.
 
The experiment follows a static FJSS design to isolate the zero-shot reasoning
capabilities of the LLMs. A dynamic scenario (e.g., machine failures) is
explicitly excluded from the scope of this work, as meaningful LLM reasoning
in such settings would require failure-specific context (e.g., failure
probabilities per machine) that goes beyond the standard FJSS formulation and
cannot be addressed within the time frame of the Masterseminar.
 
Key experimental variables are:
 
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Input representation:} Structured JSON format vs.\ natural
    language (addresses RQ2).
  \item \textbf{Prompt design:} Basic zero-shot instructions
    vs.\ Chain-of-Thought (CoT) (addresses RQ2).
\end{itemize}



\subsection{Data}

Benchmark instances are loaded directly via job-shop-lib using
load\_benchmark\_instance() (e.g., Taillard's
instances~\cite{taillardBenchmarksBasicScheduling1993}) or generated randomly via
GeneralInstanceGenerator. Due dates are not part of the original
Taillard format and are therefore generated synthetically using a slack factor
relative to each job's total processing time.
 
The system state provided to the LLM at each decision step includes:
 
\begin{itemize}
  \item Available machines and their status (idle, busy and
    expected finish times)
  \item Jobs waiting for processing
  \item Remaining operations per job
  \item Due dates
\end{itemize}
 
This state is provided to the LLM in different representations, namely
structured JSON format and natural language descriptions.

\subsection{Planned Model / Experiment / Analysis}

The experiments follow a factorial design focusing on the static FJSS to
isolate the reasoning capabilities of the models. At each step, the
Dispatcher exposes the set of ready operations; the active strategy
selects one and calls dispatch() to advance the simulation. Invalid
or conflicting actions are discarded, recorded for evaluation, and replaced by
an EDD fallback to ensure the simulation always terminates.
 
The factorial design covers the following factors:
 
\begin{itemize}
  \item Input representation: JSON vs.\ Text
  \item Prompt design: Basic vs.\ Chain-of-Thought
\end{itemize}
 
Each experimental configuration is repeated multiple times to ensure
robustness of the results. The analysis compares the LLM performance across
configurations and against the heuristic baseline, including a qualitative
analysis of typical error patterns (e.g., machine eligibility violations,
precedence errors).

\subsection{Planned Evaluation}
The evaluation is based on standard performance metrics from production scheduling and LLM-specific metrics:

\textbf{Efficiency and Scheduling Metrics:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Optimality Gap:} The relative difference between the LLM's $C_{max}$ and the CP-SAT optimum.
    \item \textbf{Heuristic Improvement:} Performance gain/loss relative to the EDD baseline.
    \item \textbf{Makespan ($C_{max}$):} Total completion time.
    \item \textbf{Tardy Jobs:} Number of jobs exceeding their due date.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{LLM-specific and Robustness Metrics:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Feasibility Rate:} Rate of invalid decisions (e.g., precedence violations).
    \item \textbf{Technical Robustness:} Rate of invalid output formats or invalid/conflicting assignments.
    \item \textbf{Latency:} Response time per decision.
\end{itemize}

In addition to quantitative evaluation, the robustness and consistency of the
LLM are analyzed by examining repeated runs under identical conditions.
A comparison with the random dispatcher, EDD, and CP-SAT reference provides
a baseline for assessing the effectiveness of the LLM-based approach.
